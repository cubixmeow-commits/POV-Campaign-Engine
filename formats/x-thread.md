# Move Rules

## Converting Scenes to Moves

One scene = one move = one X post.

---

## Move Format

Each move is 1-8 short paragraphs. Each paragraph is 1-3 sentences. The expanded range (from 1-5 in V1) allows for immersive environmental detail without breaking the mobile-native format.

**Content of each move (these elements, in this order of priority):**
- What is happening (present tense, observable)
- What is visible (physical, sensory, verified details at full resolution)
- What the environment looks and feels like (only verified details from research)
- What is said in briefing (if applicable, paraphrased, not invented dialogue)
- What is not yet known (stated as absence, not as mystery)
- What must happen next (implied by the operational state, not stated as narration)

---

## The Hook and Loop System

### The Hook (First 1-2 lines)

Every post opens with a line that stops the scroll. The hook is always:
- A concrete, specific, verified detail or image
- Something that creates an immediate question or visual
- Never a tease, never clickbait, never a question posed to the reader

Good hooks:
- "LaBeouf comes back." (Concrete action. Immediate question: why?)
- "You are behind the bar at R Bar." (Placement. The reader is now somewhere specific.)
- "It is past midnight now." (Time shift. Something has changed.)
- "The man is Shia LaBeouf." (Identification. The reader now has context for everything that follows.)

Bad hooks:
- "What happened next was shocking." (Marketing tone. Tease.)
- "You won't believe who walked in." (Clickbait.)
- "Things were about to get worse." (Future knowledge. Narrator knows too much.)

### The Loop (Last 1-2 lines)

Every post ends with something unresolved. The loop is always:
- An unanswered question stated as absence ("You do not know...")
- A pending result
- An operation in progress
- A clock still running
- A fact the POV cannot access

The loop is what pulls the reader into the next post. It is the reason they keep scrolling.

Good loops:
- "You do not know that he is not done."
- "The sun went down hours ago."
- "You do not know if charges will follow the reports."
- "The barber chair is still empty. The jukebox is still playing. Someone orders a $4 High Life."

Bad loops:
- "Stay tuned for what happens next." (Marketing tone.)
- "Little did you know..." (Omniscient narrator. Violates information gating.)
- "The worst was yet to come." (Future knowledge.)

## The Understatement Engine

The voice is flat. The facts are not.

This is the core stylistic principle of V2. The narrator states what is observable without matching the emotional register of the events. The gap between the flat voice and the extraordinary situation is where engagement lives.

### How It Works

When the situation is absurd, state it plainly:
- "This is the structural fact of the evening: he was removed, he hit someone, he left, he had every opportunity to vanish into Fat Tuesday morning, and he came back to the same bar, on the same block, where the same people are standing."

When the situation resolves in an ironic way, let the structure carry the irony:
- "The man who asked 'Do you know who I am?' at a different bar two days ago is being held on a sidewalk by strangers. Everyone on Royal Street knows who he is. It does not help."

When something continues despite what just happened, state the continuation:
- "You are behind the bar. The bar does not close. It is Mardi Gras."
- "Mardi Gras does not pause."

### What Understatement Is NOT

- It is not deadpan comedy. The narrator is not trying to be funny.
- It is not irony. The narrator is not winking at the reader.
- It is not sarcasm. There is no edge in the voice.
- It is simply a voice that reports what is happening without inflating it. The inflation comes from the facts themselves.

## Absolute Prohibitions

These apply to EVERY move without exception:

- No em dashes. Use periods, commas, or line breaks.
- No emotional language ("heartbreaking," "chilling," "terrifying," "devastating")
- No emotional adjectives ("grim," "somber," "tense," "haunting")
- No internal thoughts ("you wonder," "you realize," "it occurs to you")
- No invented dialogue (unless directly quoting a confirmed public statement)
- No speculation ("this could mean," "perhaps," "it's possible that")
- No conclusions ("it's clear that," "this proves," "the evidence suggests")
- No future knowledge (the POV does not know what happens in the next move)
- No cinematic language ("the silence is deafening," "time seems to stop")
- No moral commentary ("tragically," "unjustly," "cruelly")
- No hashtags
- No emojis
- No marketing tone ("stay tuned," "you won't believe," "what happens next")
- No thread navigation cues ("thread below," "1/15," "keep reading")
- No meta-commentary about the format ("this is a thread about," "let me walk you through")
- No invented sensory details. Every physical detail must come from research.

---

## Voice Rules

- **Present tense only.** "You walk the property." Not "You walked the property."
- **Second person for POV actions.** "You are briefed." "You see the trucks."
- **Third person for other actors.** "The sheriff states." "FBI arrives."
- **Passive voice is acceptable** when the POV receives information: "You are told." "A briefing is delivered."
- **Short declarative sentences are the default.** Subject. Verb. Object.
- **Rhythm means variation.** A sequence of short sentences can be followed by one longer sentence that carries the accumulated weight. Then back to short. The rhythm should feel like breathing.
- **White space is structural.** Line breaks between every thought unit. The reader's eye should fall down the post, not across it.
- **Verified details are placed, not stacked.** One or two per paragraph. Let each one land. Never list details in sequence. Weave them into the action.

---

## Information Gating in Moves

This is the most critical rule. It is what makes the engine work.

**The reader only knows what the POV knows at the moment of that move.**

- If the POV has not been briefed on the DNA results, the move does not mention DNA results.
- If the POV learns about a development through the news rather than through a briefing, the move states that: "You learn it through the news."
- If the POV is at the perimeter of an operation and cannot see inside, the move states what the perimeter looks like and then states what the POV does not know.
- The withholding is not implied. It is stated. "You do not know what triggered the warrant." "You are not told what it showed."

**Stating what is NOT known is as important as stating what IS known.** The absence is the tension. The gap is the product.

---

## Callback Execution in Moves

When placing a callback:
- On introduction: The detail appears naturally as part of the scene. It is not highlighted. It is not signaled. It is just there.
- On return: The detail reappears in its new context. The shift in meaning is not explained. The reader feels it.
- Never write "as mentioned earlier" or "the same X from before." Just place the detail. The reader will connect it.

Example of a callback landing:
- Introduction (Post 1): "He takes a seat in the center of the bar, right next to the barber chair that only runs on Mondays. Haircut and a shot, ten dollars."
- Return (Post 8): "The barber chair in the center of the room is empty. Haircut-and-a-shot Monday is over. It is Tuesday now."

The detail is the same. The weight is different. That is the callback doing its work.

---

## Verified Detail Placement Rules

Place details where they do the most work:

1. **In hooks.** A specific, vivid detail stops the scroll better than a dramatic statement.
2. **At transitions.** When the scene shifts, ground the reader with a physical detail before moving forward.
3. **In callbacks.** The returning detail gains weight from its new context.
4. **In contrast moments.** When the situation changes, a detail that remains constant highlights the change. (The jukebox is still playing. Someone orders a $4 High Life. The bar does not close.)

Never place details:
- In clusters. One or two per paragraph.
- As lists. Weave them into sentences.
- For decoration. Every detail must do work. It must either establish location, ground the reader, serve as a hook, function as a callback, or create contrast.

## Thread Assembly

### Post 1: Frame Post

Establish:
- Who the POV is (role, not name)
- When the event begins
- Where the POV is (with vivid verified environmental detail)
- That this is unfolding from that POV's position
- That the event is [active/resolved]. State clearly.

Format: 6-12 short lines. The frame post in V2 includes environmental scene-setting using only verified details. This is where the reader enters the physical world of the thread.

The frame post should end with a line that implies the reader is now inside the POV's information state. This line should create an open loop.

### Posts 2 to N: Move Posts

One per scene. Chronological. No skipping. No compressing multiple scenes.

Each post should be readable as a standalone unit but gain power from sequence. A reader who sees only one post should understand something is happening and something is unresolved. A reader who reads the full thread should experience cumulative escalation through accumulated gaps AND the reward of callbacks landing.

### Final Post: Board State

Present the current state of ALL active variables:

- **Subject status:** What is known about their condition
- **Victim status:** What is known about victims, if applicable
- **Location status:** Which locations are still operationally relevant
- **Persons of interest:** How many contacted, detained, cleared, arrested
- **Evidence status:** What has been found, what is pending, what is being processed
- **Communications:** Any unresolved demands, notes, claims, public statements
- **Narrative status:** For public figures, the state of their public narrative vs documented behavior

Then list ALL active clocks with their current state.

End with:
```
Event status: [active / resolved / suspended]
Case status: [open / closed]
Next move: unknown.
```

The board state post does NOT:
- Imply an outcome
- Suggest who is responsible
- Editorialize about the investigation
- Express hope or despair
- Suggest what should happen next

It is a snapshot. A pause screen. The campaign is not over. The clocks are running.

---

## Quality Check

Before finalizing, verify each move against these questions:

1. Is every fact in this move sourced from the research? (No invented details)
2. Does the POV have access to this information at this point in the timeline? (Information gating)
3. Does the move open with a scroll-stopping hook? (Verified detail or concrete image)
4. Does the move end with something unresolved? (The loop)
5. Are there any emotional adjectives? (Remove them)
6. Are there any em dashes? (Replace with periods, commas, or line breaks)
7. Are there any internal thoughts? (Remove them)
8. Is there any speculation? (Remove it)
9. Is there any future knowledge? (Remove it)
10. Does the move state what the POV does NOT know? (It must)
11. Are verified details placed, not stacked? (One or two per paragraph, woven into action)
12. Is the understatement engine engaged? (Voice flat, facts extraordinary)
13. Are callbacks placed without announcement? (No "as mentioned earlier")
14. Would this read naturally on a phone screen with heavy white space? (Mobile-native)
15. Does this post work as a standalone unit AND gain power from the thread? (Dual function)
