# Newsletter Format Rules

## Converting Scenes to Sections

One scene = one section. Not one post. Sections are longer, fewer per issue than posts per thread, and allowed to breathe.

A newsletter issue typically runs 4-8 sections (versus 8-20 posts in an X thread), because a reader in an inbox tolerates fewer, longer units than a reader on a feed.

---

## Section Format

Each section is 2-5 short paragraphs. Each paragraph is 2-4 sentences. This is longer than the X-thread unit (1-8 paragraphs of 1-3 sentences) because the newsletter reader has already opted into a longer read and is not scroll-competing against other content.

**Content of each section (same priority order as X-thread moves):**
- What is happening (present tense, observable)
- What is visible (physical, sensory, verified details at full resolution)
- What the environment looks and feels like (only verified details from research)
- What is said in briefing (if applicable, paraphrased, not invented dialogue)
- What is not yet known (stated as absence, not as mystery)
- What must happen next (implied by the operational state, not stated as narration)

---

## The Subject Line and Loop System

### The Subject Line (replaces the Hook)

The subject line does the job the first line of a post does on X: it has to earn the open, not the scroll. Same constraints as a hook:
- A concrete, specific, verified detail
- Never a tease, never clickbait, never a question posed to the reader

Good subject lines:
- "The warehouse manager finds out from Twitter."
- "Six hours before the outage, nobody knows yet."

Bad subject lines:
- "You won't believe what happened next" (clickbait)
- "A crisis unfolds" (vague, no concrete detail)

### The Section Loop (same function as the X-thread Loop)

Each section still ends on something unresolved, exactly as in the X-thread format. The difference is pacing: because sections are longer, the loop can be a full sentence or two rather than a single clipped line, but it must still be:
- An unanswered question stated as absence
- A pending result
- An operation in progress
- A clock still running

### The Issue-Level Loop

Unlike an X thread (which ends at the board state with no further contact until the next post), a newsletter has a subscriber relationship across issues. The final section of an issue should close the immediate scene's loop while leaving the larger event's board state open for the next issue, if the event is ongoing. If the event is resolved (historical), the final section can function as the board state described below.

## The Understatement Engine

Identical principle to the X-thread format: the voice is flat, the facts are not. Do not turn up the emotional register just because the medium allows more words. More space is not permission for more adjectives. It is permission for more verified detail, placed with the same one-or-two-per-paragraph discipline as the X-thread format.

---

## Absolute Prohibitions

Same list as the X-thread format, in full, with two additions specific to the newsletter medium:

- No em dashes.
- No emotional language or emotional adjectives.
- No internal thoughts.
- No invented dialogue.
- No speculation.
- No conclusions.
- No future knowledge.
- No cinematic language.
- No moral commentary.
- No hashtags or emojis.
- No marketing tone.
- No meta-commentary about the format.
- No invented sensory details.
- **No listicle formatting.** Bullet-point summaries defeat the pacing this format depends on. Prose only, section by section.
- **No CTA language mid-narrative.** ("Don't miss what happens next," "keep reading below.") The loop should pull the reader forward on its own.

---

## Voice Rules

Identical to the X-thread format: present tense, second person for POV actions, third person for other actors, passive voice permitted when the POV receives information, short declarative sentences as the default with rhythm variation, verified details placed rather than stacked.

The one adjustment: white space matters less structurally in a newsletter than on a mobile feed. Paragraphs can run slightly denser, but each section should still be visually separated (a rule or header break) so the reader can feel the scene change.

---

## Information Gating in Sections

Identical rule to the X-thread format, and just as load-bearing here: **the reader only knows what the POV knows at the moment of that section.** State what is not known as explicitly as what is known. The absence is still the tension. The gap is still the product. The longer form does not license front-loading context the POV doesn't have yet.

---

## Callback Execution

Identical mechanism to the X-thread format. Introduce a detail naturally, let it return later with changed weight, never announce the callback. Because a newsletter issue has fewer, longer sections, aim for 2-4 callbacks rather than 3-5. Fewer opportunities means each one has to be chosen carefully.

---

## Newsletter Assembly

### Section 1: Frame

Establish who the POV is (role, not name), when the event begins, where the POV is (with vivid verified environmental detail), and whether the event is active or resolved. Longer than an X-thread frame post: 3-5 short paragraphs rather than 6-12 lines. Ends with a loop.

### Sections 2 to N: Scene Sections

One per scene, chronological, no compressing multiple scenes into one section.

### Final Section: Board State (or Sign-off, if issue is ongoing)

If the event is resolved: identical structure to the X-thread board state (subject status, location status, persons of interest, evidence status, communications, active clocks, event/case status, "next move: unknown"). No implied outcome, no editorializing.

If the event is still active and this is one issue in a series: close with what remains unresolved and note plainly that the next issue will pick up where this one leaves off. Do not tease what that will contain.

---

## Quality Check

Same fifteen checks as the X-thread format apply here, substituting "section" for "move" and "subject line" for "hook." One newsletter-specific addition:

16. Does this section earn 2-5 paragraphs, or is it padded to hit a length? (Newsletter format is more forgiving of length than X threads, which makes padding easier to miss. If a scene only has one paragraph of real material, it gets one paragraph.)
