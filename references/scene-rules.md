# Scene Rules

## Breaking Acts into Scenes

Each act contains 3-6 scenes. Each scene is a playable encounter.

### Scene Definition Format

```
### Scene [Act].[Number]: [Functional Label]

- **Location:** [L-reference]
- **Trigger:** [What causes this scene to occur. An event, a briefing, an arrival.]
- **Observable inputs:** [What the POV physically sees, hears, or is told. Bullet list.]
- **Verified details available:** [Which harvested details can be placed in this scene. List from research.]
- **Decision pressure:** [What operational tension exists for the POV in this moment]
- **Information withheld:** [What the POV does NOT know. Bullet list.]
- **End condition:** [What changes when this scene ends. What is now different.]
- **Callback placement:** [If a callback is introduced or lands in this scene, note it here.]
```

### Scene Escalation Requirements

Every scene must do at least one of:
1. **Collapse an illusion.** Eliminate a hypothesis or assumption.
2. **Increase uncertainty.** Introduce new unknowns or complications.
3. **Narrow geography.** Contract the physical scope of the investigation/event.
4. **Introduce a new information bottleneck.** Create a point where critical knowledge is blocked.

If a proposed scene does none of these, it is not a scene. It is filler. Cut it.

### Observable Inputs Rules

- Only include what the POV can physically perceive or is directly told
- Do not include information from sources the POV cannot access
- Briefing content must be realistic for the POV's clearance/role level
- News and media content the POV might see on a phone or TV counts as observable
- Other actors' public statements count if the POV would plausibly encounter them
- Do NOT include the POV's emotional reaction to inputs
- V2 addition: Include verified environmental details the POV would naturally perceive. If the POV is a bartender, they see the barber chair, the jukebox, the video poker machines. These are observable inputs, not decoration.

### Information Withheld Rules

- Must be specific, not vague
- "What the FBI found inside" is specific
- "Various things" is not
- The withheld information must be REAL information that exists somewhere in the system but is inaccessible to the POV
- Structurally unknowable information (things no one knows) should be labeled as such
- The withholding must be structural (role-based, clearance-based, timing-based), not arbitrary

### Decision Pressure Rules

- Decision pressure is about what the POV must do, decide, or endure, not about what is dramatic
- If the POV has no operational decision to make and no role-based tension to endure, the decision pressure may be positional: the POV's access is narrowing, or their role is shifting
- Do not manufacture false dilemmas
- "The deputy must decide whether to call for backup" is valid if the situation warrants it
- "The deputy feels torn" is not valid (emotional interiority)

### End Condition Rules

- Must describe a concrete change in state
- The change can be: new information received, access gained or lost, geographic scope changed, actor entered or exited, resource deployed or exhausted, hypothesis confirmed or eliminated
- "The scene ends" is not an end condition
- "The deputy is now outside the inner investigation" IS an end condition

### Scene Pacing

- Early acts: scenes can be slightly longer (more setup, more observable detail, more environmental immersion)
- Middle acts: scenes should accelerate (more information per scene, faster transitions, detail becomes sharper and more specific)
- Final act: scenes should contract (tighter geography, higher density, more withheld information, callbacks landing)

The final scene of the campaign should NOT resolve. It should present the current state and stop. The POV remains in the field. The clocks are running. The next move is unknown.
