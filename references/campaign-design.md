# Campaign Design

## POV Selection

### Selection Criteria (in order of priority)

1. **Maximum uncertainty zone.** The best POV knows more than the general public but less than the lead decision-makers. This creates natural tension without requiring manufactured suspense.

2. **Physical access to multiple locations.** The POV must be able to move between at least 3-4 locations in the event space. A POV trapped in one location produces a static campaign.

3. **Exposure to escalation beats.** The POV must experience most beats either firsthand (present at the location) or through structured information delivery (briefings, announcements, news). A mix of both is ideal. Some beats experienced directly, others learned secondhand, creating an information texture.

4. **Natural information gating.** The POV's job, role, or position must organically limit what they can know. A first responder can see the scene but not the lab results. A staffer can see the orders but not the deliberation. A reporter can see the press conference but not the investigation. The gating must be structural, not arbitrary.

### Selection Anti-Patterns

Do NOT select:
- **The victim.** Requires emotional interiority. Violates the constraint against internal thoughts.
- **The perpetrator/suspect.** Requires knowledge the audience should not have. Collapses information gating.
- **The omniscient observer.** No information gaps means no tension.
- **The pure outsider.** Too far from the event to experience escalation beats directly.
- **A child or minor.** Ethical constraint.

### POV Justification Format

Justify selection in 3-5 bullets addressing ONLY:
- Access to information
- Exposure to tension
- Movement across locations
- Ability to experience escalation beats

No emotional reasoning. No "this would be compelling" or "readers would connect with."

## Player Constraints

For the selected POV, define five lists:

### 1. Accessible Locations
Which L-references the POV can physically or digitally access. Include timing notes (e.g., "accessible from Beat 1 through Beat 4, then closed").

### 2. Inaccessible Locations
Which L-references the POV cannot access and why (jurisdiction, clearance, distance, digital access).

### 3. Artifact Visibility
Three tiers:
- **Visible:** POV can see/handle the artifact directly
- **Delayed:** POV learns about the artifact through briefing or news, with a time lag
- **Invisible:** POV never accesses this artifact within the campaign

### 4. Clock Visibility
Two tiers:
- **Visible:** POV is aware of this time pressure
- **Invisible:** POV does not know this clock is running

### 5. Information Gating Matrix
Four categories:
- **Immediately visible:** Known from the start or learned in real time
- **Unlockable:** Learned through briefings, releases, or field operations at a specific beat
- **Permanently hidden:** Never accessible within this POV
- **Structurally unknowable:** No actor in the system has confirmed access to this information

## Act Structure

### Grouping Beats into Acts

Group escalation beats into 3-6 acts. An act boundary occurs when:
- The type of case changes (e.g., missing person becomes abduction)
- The information environment fundamentally shifts (e.g., goes from closed investigation to public manhunt)
- The geographic scope expands or contracts significantly
- The power balance between actors shifts
- A major illusion collapses

### Act Definition Format

For each act:

```
### ACT [Roman numeral]: [Functional Title]

**Beats:** [list]
**What fundamentally changes:** [one sentence]
**What illusion collapses:** [one sentence describing what assumption or hypothesis is eliminated]
**What tension increases:** [bullet list of specific pressures that escalate]
**What remains unresolved:** [bullet list of open questions at act end]
```

Act titles must be functional, not dramatic. "Scene Classification" not "Into the Darkness." "Signal Pollution" not "A Web of Lies."

### Structural Pivot

Identify the single moment (one beat) after which the case is no longer the same kind of case. This is not the most dramatic moment. It is the moment where the structural dynamics change irreversibly.

State:
- Which beat
- What was true before this beat
- What is true after this beat
- Why the change is irreversible

### Analytical Identifications

Identify:
- **Highest-information-density act:** The act where the most new data enters the system. Note whether the data is reliable or noisy.
- **Most isolating act for the POV:** The act where the POV has the least access to critical knowledge relative to other actors. This is often the highest-tension act because the POV can see activity but not content.

## Callback Planning (V2)

After completing the act structure, review the harvested detail list and plan 3-5 callbacks.

### What Makes a Good Callback

A good callback is a detail that:
- Appears naturally in its first context (not forced, not signaled)
- Reappears in a later context where its meaning has shifted
- Creates resonance without requiring explanation
- Rewards the thread reader who has been following from the start

### Callback Planning Format

For each planned callback:

```
**Callback [n]: [Detail]**
- Introduced in: [Post number, context]
- Returns in: [Post number, context]
- Shift in meaning: [What changed between appearances]
```

### Examples of Effective Callbacks

- A sidewalk where a celebrity poses for photos becomes the sidewalk where he is held down by strangers
- A barber chair mentioned in the opening frame sits empty in the final act
- A phrase someone used at one bar ("Do you know who I am?") echoes when they are restrained at a different bar
- Operating hours mentioned casually become relevant when the event runs past them
- A detail about sobriety stated early by the subject is contradicted by documented evidence later

### Callback Rules

- Maximum 5 per thread. Restraint matters.
- Never announce the callback. The reader must feel it, not be told about it.
- The detail must be real. Sourced from research. Never invented for the purpose of creating a callback.
- The shift in meaning must be organic. It must arise from the events, not from narrative manipulation.
